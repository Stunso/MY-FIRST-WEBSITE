THE SUBSCAPULARIS MUSCLE

The subscapularis muscle is the largest muscle of the rotator cuff group, located on the anterior (front) side of the scapula (shoulder blade). It originates from the subscapular fossa of the scapula and inserts onto the lesser tubercle of the humerus (upper arm bone). Its primary function is internal rotation of the arm, but it also helps to stabilize the shoulder joint, preventing the humerus from slipping out of the socket. Anatomy Location: Situated on the front of the scapula, it is the only rotator cuff muscle that is positioned on the anterior side of the shoulder. Origin: It arises from the entire subscapular fossa, the broad depression on the front surface of the scapula. Insertion: Its tendon inserts onto the lesser tubercle of the humerus, contributing to its strong attachment to the upper arm bone. Innervation: It is innervated by the upper and lower subscapular nerves, which arise from the posterior cord of the brachial plexus. Function Internal Rotation: The subscapularis is the strongest internal rotator of the shoulder, essential for actions like turning your arm inward or lifting it across your chest. Shoulder Stabilization: As a key component of the rotator cuff, it helps to center the head of the humerus within the glenoid cavity, preventing instability and dislocation. Adduction: It also contributes to the adduction (moving the arm towards the body) of the arm, especially in certain positions.
